---

# 1) `config.py` — The Messaging Blueprint

---

## What It Does

Defines the **topology of your messaging system**.

It stores:

* exchanges
* exchange types
* queues
* bindings
* routing rules

---

## Why It Exists

Without this:

* names get hardcoded everywhere
* changing infrastructure becomes painful

With it:

* topology is centralized
* dynamic setup becomes possible

---

## Its Role

```text id="cfg001"
Defines HOW messages should flow
```

It does not send messages.

It describes the broker structure.

---

---

# 2) `connection.py` — Broker Access Layer

---

## What It Does

Creates connection/channel objects.

This is your gateway into RabbitMQ.

---

## Why It Exists

Instead of opening broker connections in every file, you standardize access.

---

## Its Role

```text id="conn001"
Handles communication pipe to RabbitMQ
```

---

---

# 3) `router.py` — Infrastructure Initializer

---

## What It Does

Reads topology config and creates:

* exchanges
* queues
* bindings

inside RabbitMQ.

---

## Why It Exists

So broker structure is automatically created by code.

No manual setup.

---

## Its Role

```text id="route001"
Builds broker topology dynamically
```

---

---

# 4) `publisher.py` — Message Sender

---

## What It Does

Takes business payload and sends it to broker.

It handles:

* serialization
* exchange targeting
* routing key selection
* delivery settings

---

## Why It Exists

So business logic never touches RabbitMQ internals directly.

---

## Its Role

```text id="pub001"
Converts application events into broker messages
```

---

---

# 5) `consumer.py` — Queue Listener

---

## What It Does

Subscribes to a queue and processes incoming messages.

It manages:

* callbacks
* acknowledgments
* prefetching

---

## Why It Exists

Standardizes how workers consume messages.

---

## Its Role

```text id="cons001"
Receives messages from broker and hands them to handlers
```

---

---

# 6) `services/*.py` — Business Event Layer

---

## What It Does

Represents domain-specific actions.

Examples:

* payment success
* campaign launched
* contacts imported

---

## Why It Exists

Business logic should speak business language.

Not messaging language.

---

## Its Role

```text id="svc001"
Maps business actions to messaging events
```

# Why This Design Is Production-Grade

Because it separates:

---

## Infrastructure Concerns

Handled by:

* config
* router
* connection

---

---

## Messaging Concerns

Handled by:

* publisher
* consumer

---

---

## Business Concerns

Handled by:

* services

---

---

# The Principle Behind It

```text id="pr001"
Business code should not care about broker mechanics
Broker code should not contain business rules
```

That separation keeps systems scalable.

---

# One-Line Summary of Each File

| File            | Purpose                           |
| --------------- | --------------------------------- |
| `config.py`     | Defines broker structure          |
| `connection.py` | Opens RabbitMQ access             |
| `router.py`     | Creates exchanges/queues/bindings |
| `publisher.py`  | Sends messages                    |
| `consumer.py`   | Receives messages                 |
| `services/*.py` | Business-specific events          |

---

# Final Mental Model

The codebase is layered so that:

* **topology is declarative**
* **broker access is reusable**
* **messaging is abstracted**
* **business logic stays clean**

That’s what makes it production-level—not the syntax, but the architecture behind it.
